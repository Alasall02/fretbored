The most important scale in guitar. Every riff you love — Maggot Brain, blues, funk, rock — lives here. Two notes per string, 5 strings. Learn position 1 (shown) first and you're 80% of the way there.
How to use this scale
Root notes (gold) are your home base — land on these and it sounds resolved.
Other notes (white) are the flavor — move between them freely.

Start slow: go up the scale, then down. Then jump between notes randomly. Then play over a backing track (see Tracks tab) and target the gold root notes on chord changes.

Technique Library
Step-by-step guides for every skill in your plan
1
Find the bend note
Start on the G string, 7th fret (D note). This is the classic blues/funk bend position. Use your ring finger to fret it.
2
Stack your fingers for support
Place your middle and index fingers behind the ring finger on the same string. All three fingers push together — this is where the power comes from, not just one finger.
3
Push toward the ceiling
Rotate your wrist (not just your fingers) to push the string upward. A full-step bend means the pitch rises by 2 frets. Target the sound of the 9th fret note.
4
Hold and release slowly
Bend to the peak, hold it for 1-2 seconds (feel it sing), then slowly release back to the original pitch. That slow release is the emotional core of blues and Maggot Brain.
5
Practice target
Bend, hold, release — 10 times cleanly. Then add it to the end of a pentatonic phrase. The bend should feel like a sentence ending with a question mark.
1
Fret a note cleanly
Pick any note on the B or high e string. Use your ring finger and support with middle and index behind it.
2
Micro-bend oscillation
Push the string slightly sharp (small bend), then release back to pitch, then slightly sharp again. This is the oscillation. Think of a singer holding a note and wavering — that is vibrato.
3
Wrist drives the motion
Your wrist rotation powers vibrato, not your fingers. Fingers hold the note; wrist oscillates. This is the same motion as turning a doorknob back and forth quickly.
4
Control the speed
Slow vibrato (2-3 oscillations/second) = vocal, expressive, emotional. Fast vibrato = nervous energy, urgency. Learn slow first. Eddie Hazel uses slow wide vibrato on Maggot Brain.
1
Right hand position
Thumb (p) covers strings E, A, D. Index (i) covers G. Middle (m) covers B. Ring (a) covers high e. Rest your wrist lightly near the sound hole — don't plant it on the body.
2
The basic pattern
Thumb → Index → Middle → Ring → Middle → Index. Written: p-i-m-a-m-i. Hold a G chord and repeat this loop until it feels automatic. Say it out loud as you play.
3
Thumb is the anchor
The thumb always hits the root note of whatever chord you're on. G chord → thumb hits 6th string. C chord → thumb hits 5th string. D chord → thumb hits 4th string.
4
Move through chord changes
Once the pattern is automatic on one chord, add G → C → Am → D with the full pattern continuing through the change. Don't stop the pattern when your chord hand moves.
5
This is Banana Pancakes
This exact pattern, played over the G → C → Am → D progression, is the intro and verse of Banana Pancakes. You're learning fingerstyle and the song simultaneously.
1
What is a montuno
The montuno is the repeated rhythmic cell that drives salsa and son cubano. On guitar it's short, percussive, off-beat strums — choppy and tight, not smooth.
2
The rhythm pattern
Count: 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 +. Strum on the "and" of beat 2 and the "and" of beat 3. The downbeats (1, 2, 3, 4) are often silent — the accents are on the off-beats. This syncopation is the feel of salsa.
3
Mute between strums
After each strum, lightly rest your strumming hand on the strings to mute them. The mute is part of the sound — it creates the choppy, percussive texture. Practice: strum, mute, strum, mute.
4
Practice over clave
Open the Tracks tab → Salsa Clave Backing. Play your montuno pattern over it at 100 BPM first. Feel how the guitar locks with the clave. When it clicks, raise the BPM to 120.
1
The core progression
Am – G – F – E. That's it. Every classic bachata song is built on some version of this minor key progression. The E chord at the end creates tension that resolves back to Am — that longing sound IS bachata.
2
The strum pattern
Down – Down – Down – Down – Up, with a slight accent and pause on beat 4. Count: 1-2-3-4(pause)-up. The "4" beat gets a slight accent and there's a tiny hesitation before the up strum — that rhythmic hiccup is the bachata feel.
3
Two beats per chord
Start by giving each chord two full beats before changing. Am (2 beats) → G (2 beats) → F (2 beats) → E (2 beats). Once that's automatic, you can play 4 beats per chord for a slower, more spacious feel.
4
Play over a drum track
Open the Tracks tab → Bachata Drum Track. Start at 90 BPM. The percussion tells you exactly where beat 1 is — lock to it. When it feels natural, raise to 100, then 120 BPM.
1
What is a power chord
A power chord is just 2 notes: the root and the 5th. No 3rd, which means no major or minor quality — it works over everything. This is why rock, punk, and metal are built on them.
2
The shape
Index finger on the root note (any fret on E or A string). Ring finger two frets up, one string down. That's it. E5 = index on 6th string open (or fret 0), ring on 5th string 2nd fret.
3
Move it anywhere
The shape is the same everywhere on the neck. Slide the index to the 5th fret on the E string = A5. 7th fret = B5. The letter follows whatever note your index is on.
4
Add a little gain
Power chords come alive with overdrive or distortion. On your electric, turn the gain up slightly. Strum just the 2 fretted strings — avoid hitting open strings above or below. Palm muting adds a tight, punchy sound.
